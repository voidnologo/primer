why now

The floor moved.

For a decade, the moat was writing good code.

That skill just got commoditized. An assistant writes it faster than I can.

So the value moves up the stack — to the judgment, architecture, and decisions an LLM can't make on its own.

The quiet question under it:

“Tens of thousands of strong engineers are on the market. If I were too — what makes me distinguishable?”

Staying distinguishable is the job now. That takes deliberate growth — not whatever work happens to hand you.

Demand-driven

Generic & stale

A search engine, a chatbot, and a stack of bookmarks don't fix any of these. They make the feeling of progress cheap and the substance rare.

what it is

A senior peer who happens to know your whole history.

A Claude Code skill. You type /primer <topic>; it runs a calibrated, narrative lesson — and writes down what it learned about you. Three rules it never breaks:

01

Senior peer, not teacher

Talks to you like a colleague at a meetup. No cheerleading. Disagrees when you're wrong — and holds the line under pushback.

02

Struggle > fluent answers

Makes you predict or derive before it explains. You build the idea in your own head — the part that actually sticks.

03

Current & grounded

Every technical claim tagged verified via docs or from-training. Never teaches a take the field has moved past.

Calibrated to you — and sharper the more you use it.

how it works · 2 of 3 — a lesson

Every session runs the same loop.

Elicitwhat's your model now?
▸
Probepredict before I explain
▸
Diagnosewhere's the real edge?
▸
Deepenworked → faded → free
▸
Recapyou summarize; prompts out

It opens by asking what you already believe — then probes you with a real failure scenario and waits. No auto-complete. It teaches only at your edge: skip what you know, slow down where you're fuzzy.

It closes by handing you the pen — you put the takeaway in your own words (that's the encoding), and the session emits retrieval prompts for spaced review later.

PRIMER
Probe-first is the heart. Contrast with "ask ChatGPT and get the answer instantly" — that skips the part that builds you.
how it works · 3 of 3 — it learns you

It tracks what it has seen vs. what it assumed.

Every topic carries a depth marker with confidence + evidence — never a certain-looking guess:

async event loop confidence: low
↳ intake probe: reasoned well, but the
   blocking-freezes-the-loop invariant isn't locked

outbox pattern confidence: high
↳ derived it live, sketched the schema

Confidence moves both ways — and the loop isn't allowed to just grade itself:

  • ↑ You correct its mistake → strongest signal of mastery.
  • ↓ You blank on a cold-recall prompt → confidence drops, topic requeues.
  • ⏳ Untouched too long → it decays toward "needs review." Knowledge you don't retrieve, you lose.

So the picture of you gets truer with use — not quietly more optimistic.

why not just ask the chatbot

Because a fluent answer is the trap.

Ask a chatbot to “explain X” and you get a perfect paragraph. You nod. You feel sharper. You retain almost none of it — nothing was built in your head.

Primer is designed against exactly that:

  • Makes you produce the idea before it confirms it.
  • Cold-recall & spaced review — Anki-importable prompts from every lesson.
  • Honest about its own confidence — flags guesses, won't fake authority.
  • Currency as a rule — searches for current sources every lesson; a stale-list is the guardrail. event-source everything Alpine by default

The goal isn't a good answer in the moment. It's a better engineer over time — and better at learning, too.
